Technical Program Management at Xbox

Coordinating cross-functional program work for Xbox Cloud Gaming — tracking timelines, dependencies, and milestones while synthesizing player research into feature recommendations presented to senior stakeholders.

Company Microsoft / Xbox
Role TPM Intern
Timeline May – Aug 2025
Focus area Xbox Cloud Gaming, server hardware & OS platform

Context

Xbox Cloud Gaming runs across complex hardware, OS platform, cloud infrastructure, and product workstreams — with engineering, product, design, and research teams all moving in parallel. As a TPM intern, I supported cross-functional programs where these teams needed shared visibility and clear delivery paths to hit milestones on time.

Problem

Multiple active workstreams had moving timelines, evolving requirements, and technical dependencies that spanned teams with very different cadences. The challenge was keeping everyone aligned while translating technical progress — and player research findings — into clear, decision-ready updates for stakeholders who needed to act on them.

My role

I coordinated timelines, tracked requirements and dependencies, synthesized player research, and prepared program updates and feature recommendations for senior stakeholders. My work sat at the intersection of engineering delivery and user insight — keeping programs on track while ensuring player perspectives shaped product direction.

What I did

  • Coordinated cross-functional program work across engineering, product, design, and research teams — maintaining shared visibility into milestones, risks, and dependencies
  • Tracked requirements and dependencies across workstreams to surface blockers early and keep delivery schedules accurate
  • Conducted player interviews and usability research to identify pain points and translate findings into structured product recommendations
  • Prepared and presented program status updates and feature recommendations to senior stakeholders in a clear, decision-ready format
  • Maintained integrated timelines, risk logs, and status summaries across multiple active programs

Outcome & what I learned

This experience strengthened how I communicate across technical and non-technical teams, organize ambiguous workstreams, and turn research and technical details into clear next steps. I left with direct, hands-on experience in cross-functional TPM at enterprise scale — and a sharper sense of how program management creates the conditions for teams to do their best work.

Academic research · Loyola Marymount University

Modernizing GRNsight — A Scientific Visualization Tool

Modernizing CI infrastructure, migrating from CommonJS to ES Modules, and improving long-term maintainability for an open-source scientific visualization tool used by biology researchers.

Project GRNsight (open source)
Role Research Assistant & Engineer
Timeline Jan 2024 – April 2025
Tools JavaScript, Node.js, Express, D3.js, GitHub Actions

Context

GRNsight is an open-source web application developed at LMU that lets biology researchers upload, visualize, and interact with gene regulatory networks — the systems that control how genes are expressed in living organisms. Researchers and graduate students depend on it as part of their scientific workflow, so both reliability and usability matter. When I joined, the codebase had accumulated technical debt that was slowing development and making it harder for new contributors to onboard.

Problem

There were no automated checks on pull requests, linting was inconsistent, and there was no CI pipeline to catch regressions before they merged. For an open-source scientific tool, that's a real risk: a broken merge could affect researchers in the middle of active work. At the same time, the D3.js visualization interface had responsiveness issues and edge cases in how it handled certain network structures. The tool needed infrastructure investment and interface refinement at the same time.

My role

Research assistant with dual focus: improve the engineering infrastructure that contributors rely on, and enhance the interface that researchers interact with. Moving between two very different user groups — developers who need predictable workflows, and scientists who need a tool that renders their data correctly — required understanding both well enough to make tradeoffs between them.

Stakeholders & users

  • Biology researchers and graduate students — primary end users relying on the tool for active scientific work
  • Faculty research advisors — directing technical and scientific priorities for the project
  • Open-source contributors — developers who needed reliable, documented tooling to contribute safely

Process

  • Audited the existing Node.js and Express codebase to identify friction points in the development and contribution workflow
  • Migrated the codebase from CommonJS to ES Modules to align with modern JavaScript standards and improve long-term maintainability
  • Implemented GitHub Actions for automated CI: linting checks, test validation, and PR status gates
  • Refactored D3.js data visualization components to handle edge cases in network data more gracefully
  • Improved responsiveness of the web interface across different screen sizes and dataset sizes
  • Contributed to technical documentation to lower the barrier for new contributors and preserve institutional knowledge

Key decisions & tradeoffs

The most consequential call was prioritizing CI automation before interface improvements. It would have been faster to start with visible user-facing work — but unreliable tooling meant any interface change I made could be quietly broken by a later merge. Getting automated checks in place first made every subsequent contribution, including my own, more trustworthy. For the CI setup itself, I chose GitHub Actions because it required no additional infrastructure and integrated directly into the existing PR workflow.

Challenges

Working on a scientific tool means your users have deep domain expertise in a field you don't. Biologists know what a gene regulatory network should look like — I had to learn enough to recognize when a visualization was rendering incorrectly versus just unfamiliar. Every interface change required validating against actual research workflows, not just visual instinct. That meant more back-and-forth with the research team than a typical web project and a different bar for "done."

Outcome

The project now has an automated CI pipeline that catches linting errors and test failures before they reach the main branch. Developer workflow documentation improved contributor onboarding clarity. D3.js improvements made network rendering more stable and responsive across different screen sizes and data inputs. The work is ongoing — I'm continuing to contribute as the tool evolves.

What I learned

Modernizing a working system is fundamentally a communication problem. Every change to code that real users depend on has stakeholders — researchers who need consistent behavior, and contributors who need a predictable workflow. The best decisions I made were the ones I ran by the team before writing a line of code. I also learned that "engineering for researchers" is a genuinely different design problem — the domain context changes which tradeoffs matter most.

Leaders of Tomorrow — Digital Experience Design

Designing user-centered website and digital experience improvements for youth and family-facing programs — with a focus on clarity, accessibility, parent communication, and program visibility.

Organization Leaders of Tomorrow After School Program
Role UI/UX Designer
Status Design & content strategy phase
Tools Figma, stakeholder interviews, content mapping

Context

Leaders of Tomorrow is a youth leadership after-school program serving students and families. Their existing digital presence needed to better communicate program offerings, make it easier for parents and guardians to find information, and reflect the quality of the program itself. I'm working with the organization to map, design, and improve their digital experience from the ground up.

My role

UI/UX Designer responsible for translating program goals into a clear, accessible digital experience — working directly with stakeholders to understand what families need to find, what the organization needs to communicate, and how those two things come together in a design that works for everyone.

Currently working on

  • Conducting stakeholder interviews to understand organizational goals, communication priorities, and the information families need most
  • Mapping content structure and information architecture for the program website
  • Designing wireframes and page layouts with an emphasis on accessibility and clear visual hierarchy
  • Ensuring parent communication pathways — program schedules, contact info, enrollment — are easy to navigate

Supporting business optimization by evaluating current software tools, workflows, and operational pain points to identify opportunities for automation, consolidation, and cost savings.

Organization Launch Industries
Role TPM / Systems Optimization
Status Tool evaluation & requirements gathering
Focus Software consolidation, automation, cost efficiency

Context

Launch Industries is a growing organization with an evolving software and tools footprint. As operations scale, the stack of tools, subscriptions, and workflows has grown without a clear strategy — creating redundancy, manual work, and unnecessary cost. The goal is to bring structure to that complexity: evaluate what exists, understand where the pain is, and identify where automation or consolidation creates real leverage.

My role

TPM and systems optimization contributor responsible for gathering requirements, auditing existing tools and workflows, and translating operational pain points into actionable recommendations for automation, consolidation, or replacement.

Currently working on

  • Inventorying and evaluating current software tools and subscriptions to identify redundancy and underutilization
  • Gathering requirements from stakeholders across the organization to understand workflow pain points
  • Mapping operational processes to identify manual work that could be automated or streamlined
  • Analyzing cost and overhead across the current tool stack to surface consolidation opportunities
  • Documenting findings and building a prioritized recommendation set for process and tooling improvements
Skills demonstrated Requirements gathering Stakeholder interviews Systems evaluation Process mapping Technical documentation Business analysis

Building toward

A prioritized recommendations report that gives the organization a clear path to reduce tool overhead, eliminate redundant spend, and automate the manual work that currently slows the team down.
